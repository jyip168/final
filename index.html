Summary

Nutrient content generally does not affect product pricing. Most products have a wide range of prices for similar nutrient levels which show little to no consistent patterns. Certain categories like packaged rice, jello, and baby food, may show patterns of weak correlation, but nothing strong enough to make an argument. There may also be misleading trends from limited product selections and outliers.

Story

With the increasing demand for more healthier products, stores and brands have started releasing more "healthier" alternatives to their products. Although, some companies actually charge more for their products that claim to be "healthier". The goal of this project was to explore whether the nutritional content of products influences their pricing in stores in general to get a better grasp on how products might be marked up for their health benefits.

After analyzing the data, it became clear that for most foods, nutrient levels have little to no impact on price. Most scatterplots showed products with similar nutrient content sold across a wide range of prices or formed tall vertical clusters, which denoted price did not change based on nutrients. If you were to walk into a supermarket, you would be likely to get a cheap drink with similar sugar contents as some of the more expensive drinks.

The coefficients plot is more of an estimate the impact of pricing rather than analytic evidence to prove change, if there is an impact. Estimations either range near to 0, which could mean your food could have fluctuations in pricing in the cents, or have zero impact. There are also other estimations that have more larger intervals resulting in more unreliable conclusions. Overall, realisticly most similar products aren't being compared and priced based on their contents.
